Research

Overview

My research focuses on orographic precipitation and extreme rainfall associated with tropical cyclones in Taiwan. I combine polarimetric radar observations, rain gauges, and disdrometer observations to understand precipitation processes in mountainous regions. I am generally interested in the following topics:

  • Orographic enhancement of rainfall in tropical cyclone environments
  • Polarimetric radar analysis of precipitation microphysics
  • Dual-Doppler wind retrieval and storm kinematics
  • Hydrometeor classification and quantitative precipitation estimation (QPE)
  • Polarimetric radar operator for comparison between observations and numerical models

Recent Projects

Case Study of Typhoon Chanthu (2021)

Orographic Enhancement of Isolated Shallow Convection
We investigate the orographic enhancement of precipitation as shallow convective clouds, located more than 100 km away from the center of Typhoon Chanthu, passed over the isolated Da-Tun Mountain in northern Taiwan. The enhancement mechanism appears to differ from the classical seeder-feeder process, and we aim to clarify the governing processes based on observational data.

Contribution of Background Precipitation to the Seeder-Feeder Effect
We provide observational evidence of the seeder-feeder effect associated with orographic precipitation over the isolated Da-Tun Mountain in northern Taiwan, under the influence of Typhoon Chanthu’s outer rainbands and stratiform background precipitation. This study clarifies how differences in background precipitation modulate terrain-induced precipitation enhancement, with implications for improving the prediction of localized heavy rainfall events.
